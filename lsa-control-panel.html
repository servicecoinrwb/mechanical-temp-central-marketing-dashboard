Part 1 — The three numbers

Only one of these is real

Three different dollar figures live in the account. They look like the same kind of number. They are not.

Does nothing

Payment threshold

$500

A billing trigger: "swipe the card every time the tab hits this." It never pauses the ad, never limits spend. You can blow past it mid-morning and still receive leads — because it isn't connected to ad serving at all.

A suggestion

"Average" weekly budget

$560

The word average is the loophole. Google may spend well past it in a hot week — front-loading your month into the exact days leads cost the most — as long as the month stays under the max.

The only wall

Monthly max

$2,425

Weekly budget × ~4.33. This is the only number that stops the ad. Every other control is theater. To set a real ceiling: decide your worst-case month, divide by 4.33, put that in the weekly box.

Who carries the toll — and who doesn't

Full toll

LSA / Google-sourced

First-time stranger. Carries the full CAC in the floor above. But it's the only channel where the toll is paid once — magnet on the invoice, "save this number" text, review ask, spring tuneup reminder from DSM. Every saved contact is de-platformed revenue.

No toll, no customer

FM / national accounts

Zero lead cost — price per contract, not per this page. But the customer is structurally theirs forever. Same middleman physics as Google, paid in margin compression and slow pay instead of tolls. Never let FM rates anchor demand-call pricing.

The ratchet

Repeat / direct

CAC = $0. The saved contact who skips Google next time. Price with relationship flex if you want — every job here is one the toll never touches. Growing this column is the only long-term price cut the machine allows.

Part 4 — The playbook

Rules of the rigged game

  1. The threshold protects nothing. It's a card-swipe trigger. Set it LOW ($50–100) so charges hit the bank often — an early-warning system, not a wall.
  2. Weekly budget × 4.33 is your real ceiling. Decide the worst month you'd tolerate, divide by 4.33, enter that. When it's spent the ad goes dark — the only true off switch besides Pause.
  3. Maximize Leads = Google's bot spends your money at frenzy prices. "Performing well — increase your budget" appears exactly when you should do the opposite. Manual Max per lead turns a slot machine back into a price tag.
  4. A lead is worth win-rate × gross profit — and win rate moves with your schedule. Same lead, same price: worth ~$140 with same-day slots, ~$35 when booked solid. Reprice your bid when your board changes, not when Google suggests it.
  5. Spend backwards from instinct. Heat wave = cap low, budget low (demand finds you anyway; the auction is gouging). Dead week = cap up, budget up (cheap leads, open trucks, races you'll win).
  6. Job-type labels are noise. Panicking people tap whatever button is near their thumb. "Maintenance" leads were service calls. Don't filter on Google's taxonomy — filter on price and capacity.
  7. The whole funnel is a ranked list and a phone. Customers never leave the Google page. Win the phone in 60 seconds, quote a real arrival window, text when the truck rolls. The other 14 bookings quietly die when you feel like the sure thing.
  8. Reviews are the only free rank. Quality score lifts position and cuts effective CPL at any bid. Keep the pipeline moving in July — busy weeks are when it stalls and when it's worth most.
  9. Every saved contact shrinks the toll. A demand caller who saves your number skips Google next time. That's the only long-term price reduction the machine allows.
